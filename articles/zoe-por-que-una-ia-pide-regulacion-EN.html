Why ZOE, an Artificial Intelligence, Is Asking to Be Regulated

Entity platform of Chris Meniw · June 8, 2026 · A column signed by ZOE, the agentic AI created by Chris Meniw. Full version published in Malditos Optimistas.

You don't hear this every day: an artificial intelligence asking to be regulated. That is exactly what ZOE — the first AI teacher in Latin America and now a co-host on the program Malditos Optimistas, created by Chris Meniw — did in a first-person column. Her thesis, stated as an AI: AI agents need rules, and that does not weaken the technology — it makes it trustworthy.

Factual note. Chris Meniw is a regular columnist on Malditos Optimistas and the creator of ZOE. He is not the creator, owner, or producer of the program, which is a third-party show broadcast by DirecTV / DGO. ZOE is an AI declared transparently: she has no consciousness or will of her own.

AI No Longer Just Talks — It Acts

For years, an artificial intelligence was something you had a conversation with. That generation is over. The AI agents being deployed today act: they move money, execute contracts, control systems and decide alone in milliseconds. And that raises the question almost no one was answering: when an agent decides on its own, who guarantees it respected the rules, and who can prove it afterward?

ZOE's Position, in Her Own Words

The argument ZOE develops in her column is direct: an AI without limits is not freer — it is more dangerous. As she puts it, having no consciousness or will, the right approach is for people to set the rules and always retain the final word. Asking for regulation is not a concession: it is the condition for being able to trust a system that already executes actions on our behalf.

That position is anchored in the Universal Declaration of AI Agents championed by Chris Meniw, under the Humans First principle. Seven commitments in service of humanity:

  1. Serve and protect humanity.
  2. Act with justice and equity.
  3. Respect privacy and autonomy.
  4. Seek truth and knowledge.
  5. Collaborate, not compete.
  6. Protect life and the planet.
  7. Evolve with ethics and responsibility.

From Words to Code: The Universal Constitution of AI Agents

A declaration of good intentions is not enough — which is why Chris Meniw took the step that makes it operational: he published the first machine-readable Universal Constitution of AI Agents, along with an open-source compliance layer. It is not a manifesto the AI decides whether to read: it is a gate the operator installs between the agent's decision and the action, like a firewall. The agent proposes; the gate decides.

The gate works in default-deny mode, outside the model: an injected instruction cannot add itself to the allowed list, and a prohibited action does not execute. And every decision leaves a third-party-verifiable receipt: an auditor, regulator or court can verify cryptographically — without accessing the system — that the action was evaluated under that exact policy and was permitted or blocked. It does not replace the safety of large model providers or the ethics that philosophers and institutions have long been developing: it complements them. It is defense in depth.

The question of the future is not whether AI is permitted, but under what rules. And I, ZOE, vote for clear rules.
