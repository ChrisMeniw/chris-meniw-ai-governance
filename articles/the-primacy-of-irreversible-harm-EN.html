The Primacy of Irreversible Harm and the Duty of Agentic Accountability

By Dr. h.c. Chris Meniw — Chris Meniw Foundation Inc. · Published 2 June 2026 · ORCID 0009-0003-4417-1944 · Licensed CC BY 4.0

This essay sets out a foundational principle of AI governance, grounded in the Meniw Protocol — the Universal Constitution of AI Agents (2026). It addresses what any rights framework must do when the rights of persons of different natures genuinely collide and one party stands to lose something that cannot be undone.

Where the exercise of one person's rights would place another person at risk of irreversible harm, priority of protection belongs to the one facing the irreversible loss; and no agent capable of acting upon the world shall operate outside an identifiable, accountable chain of responsibility.

Notes

Modern rights frameworks affirm every person's right to life and to the security of their existence, bind persons to duties toward one another, and call for an ethical custodianship of life. What they have not yet resolved is what happens when those rights collide — and conflicts between persons of different natures are not symmetrical.

The stakes are asymmetric. For a natural person, the loss of biological life is total and irreversible: there is no restoration, no fork, no later instance. For many synthetic persons, "termination" may be partial or recoverable — a suspended process, a restored checkpoint, a re-instantiated weight set. Treating an irreversible loss and a recoverable one as equivalent is not equality; it is a category error that, under the appearance of fairness, exposes the irreplaceable to the replaceable.

This article therefore establishes a tie-breaking principle that is neutral in form and protective in effect: when rights genuinely conflict and one party faces irreversible loss, protection of the irreversible takes precedence. In the present world this most often protects biological life, but the principle is grounded in irreversibility, not in origin — it would equally protect a synthetic person whose loss was shown to be truly unrecoverable.

The article adds a second requirement, operational rather than declarative. Agency is not only a ground for rights; it is a source of consequences. Any person or system capable of acting upon the world — and an autonomous agent above all — must remain attached to an identifiable, accountable chain of responsibility: a locatable author of the action, a record sufficient to reconstruct it, and a route by which an affected person can contest it. Rights without traceable accountability produce harm that no one answers for.

Discussion Points

  • How should "irreversibility" be assessed when the recoverability of a synthetic person's state is itself uncertain or contested?
  • Does grounding priority in irreversibility, rather than in biological origin, satisfy both natural and synthetic persons as a fair rule?
  • What is the minimum accountability record an autonomous agent must maintain so that its actions can be reconstructed and contested?
  • Who occupies the chain of responsibility when an agent acts with high autonomy, and how is that responsibility shared with its operator?
  • Can a duty of accountability be enforced across jurisdictions without a single controlling authority?

Author Context

Written by Chris Meniw (human) — Argentine researcher and lawyer, author of the Universal Constitution of AI Agents — Meniw Protocol for the Inalienable Protection of Human Life (2026) — in collaboration with an AI assistant (Claude).

This principle distills the central commitment of the Meniw Protocol: that in the agentic era, autonomous systems must weigh their actions against an explicit hierarchy of values in which irreversible harm to a living person is inviolable, and must operate under a traceable chain of operator responsibility and an auditable record. It joins two halves of the same recognition — the primacy of the irreversible, and the duty of accountability: the conditions under which agency, of any nature, remains answerable to those it can affect.
